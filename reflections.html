Jodelyn

UI Lead

Screen 5 – Mood Tracker & Diary

05
"Designing the Mood Tracker taught me how important emotional UX is."

I learned that a simple intensity slider can confuse new users without proper guidance. Heuristic evaluation opened my eyes to details I never noticed during design — like the missing validation messages when saving without selecting a mood. Going forward, I will always think about error states and empty states first before finalizing any screen design.

Error States Emotional UX Validation
👨

Julius

Developer

Screen 6 – Home Dashboard

06
"Visual clutter is invisible to the designer but obvious to the user."

Working on the Home Dashboard showed me how easy it is to create visual clutter unintentionally — I had duplicate progress rings that confused testers. Having teammates evaluate my screen gave me a perspective I could not see on my own. I now understand that consistency and user control are not optional design choices — they are fundamental HCI requirements that affect every single user interaction.

Consistency User Control Visual Hierarchy
👩

Celes

Researcher

Screen 3 – Daily Planner

03
"Real users always see your app differently than you do."

I realized that a "simple" planner screen has many hidden usability decisions — where to place the Add button, how to show completed tasks, what happens when you delete something. Testing with real respondents was the most valuable part of this project. They found issues we never expected, like the header feeling too empty and lacking a greeting. Real feedback humbles you as a designer.

Usability Testing Layout Balance User Feedback
👨

Meljun

Designer

Screen 4 – Habit Tracker

04
"Color alone is not enough — you need labels, shapes, and context."

The Habit Tracker was the most complex screen because it had to communicate multiple states — done, in progress, missed — all at once. I learned that color alone is not enough for accessibility; you also need labels and distinct shapes. The biggest lesson was about help documentation — I assumed users would intuitively understand the dot color system, but usability testing proved that assumption completely wrong.

Accessibility Multi-State UI Affordance
👨

Mark

Tester

Screen 7 – Analytics

07
"Every state of the UI needs to be designed — including the empty ones."

Analytics screens are easy to over-engineer. I focused on showing the right data at the right time — productivity trends, mood frequency, and habit consistency. The most humbling moment was realizing our empty states were completely blank. HCI2 taught me that every possible state of the UI needs to be designed, including the ones with no data. Design is not just about the happy path.

🌿 Group Takeaways

What We Learned as a Team

Four core lessons from the HCI2 design and evaluation process that will shape our future work.

🎯

Design for Real Users

Assumptions fail. Testing with 5 respondents revealed issues none of us noticed in 3 weeks of designing. Real feedback is irreplaceable.

🔍

Heuristics are a Framework

Nielsen's 10 heuristics gave us a structured lens to evaluate objectively — not just by personal preference or aesthetic taste.

♿

Accessibility is Non-Negotiable

Color contrast, tap target sizes, and icon labels are not extras — they are core requirements for all users regardless of ability.

🔄

Iteration is the Process

Design → Evaluate → Improve. The prototype we submitted is better because of evaluation, not just initial design effort.

🌿

Thank You

This project was a collaborative learning experience that combined design, research, and evaluation. The Wellness Super App is our group's commitment to building technology that genuinely supports people's wellbeing — and this evaluation has made it better.
